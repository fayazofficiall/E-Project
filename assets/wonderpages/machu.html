Machu Picchu

The ancient Incan city nestled high in the Andes

WONDERS WORLD

The Lost City of the Incas

Machu Picchu is a 15th-century Incan citadel located in the Eastern Cordillera of southern Peru, on a 2,430-meter (7,970 ft) mountain ridge. Often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas," it is one of the most famous and well-preserved archaeological sites of the Inca Empire.

Built in the mid-1400s under the reign of Inca emperor Pachacuti, Machu Picchu is an engineering marvel, featuring intricate stonework and terraces. It was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and is considered one of the most spectacular cultural sites in the world.

Quick Facts
  • Location: Cusco Region, Peru
  • Built: c. 1450
  • Founder: Inca Emperor Pachacuti
  • Elevation: 2,430 meters (7,970 feet)
  • Materials: Granite stone
  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: 1983

History of Machu Picchu

c. 1450

Inca Emperor Pachacuti orders the construction of Machu Picchu as a royal estate and religious site.

1532

The Spanish conquest of the Inca Empire begins, though Machu Picchu remains undiscovered by the conquistadors.

1572

Machu Picchu is abandoned as the last remnants of the Inca resistance fall to Spanish rule.

1911

American historian Hiram Bingham rediscovers Machu Picchu and introduces it to the world.

1983

Machu Picchu is designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its cultural and historical significance.

2007

Machu Picchu is named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World through a global poll.

Interesting Facts

Lost City of the Incas

Machu Picchu remained hidden from the outside world for centuries and was rediscovered in 1911 by Hiram Bingham.

Sun Temple

The Temple of the Sun is one of the most sacred structures in Machu Picchu, aligned with the solstices.

Cloud Forest Location

Machu Picchu is located in a cloud forest, making its ecosystem unique with rare orchids and diverse wildlife.

Inca Trail

The famous Inca Trail leads to Machu Picchu and takes hikers through stunning Andean landscapes.

Earthquake-Proof Architecture

The Incas used a special technique called "ashlar" masonry, making Machu Picchu withstand earthquakes.

Intihuatana Stone

The Intihuatana stone was used by the Incas as an astronomical clock and for spiritual rituals.

Architectural Marvel

Machu Picchu is an extraordinary example of Incan engineering, built without the use of mortar, iron, or wheels.

The site features precisely cut stones that fit together so perfectly that not even a blade can pass between them. The city is divided into urban and agricultural sectors, with terraces built to prevent erosion.

The Temple of the Sun, the Intihuatana stone, and the Room of the Three Windows are among the most important structures, showcasing the Incas' advanced knowledge of astronomy and architecture.

Location

Machu Picchu is located in the Andes Mountains of Peru, near the Urubamba River valley.
