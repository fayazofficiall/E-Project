Petra

An ancient rock-cut city, famous for its intricate architecture and water conduit system

WONDERS WORLD

A Marvel of Ancient Nabataean Architecture

Petra, also known as the "Rose City," is an ancient rock-cut city built by the Nabataeans. It is famous for its intricate architecture and advanced water conduit system.

Located in present-day Jordan, Petra was a thriving trade hub around the 4th century BC. The city was carved into pink sandstone cliffs and served as the capital of the Nabataean Kingdom. It later came under Roman rule and flourished before gradually being abandoned. Rediscovered in 1812 by Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt, Petra remains one of the most iconic archaeological sites in the world.

Quick Facts
  • Location: Southern Jordan
  • Built: Around 4th century BC
  • Known For: Rock-cut architecture, water system
  • Materials: Sandstone
  • Civilization: Nabataeans
  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: 1985

History of Petra

4th Century BC

The Nabataeans establish Petra as their capital, developing advanced water systems and rock-cut structures.

1st Century BC

Petra reaches its peak as a major trade hub, controlling caravan routes between Arabia, Egypt, and the Mediterranean.

106 AD

The Roman Empire annexes Petra, integrating it into the province of Arabia Petraea. Roman influence leads to new architectural additions.

3rd-6th Century AD

Petra gradually declines due to changing trade routes and earthquakes that damage the city's infrastructure.

7th-12th Century

Petra is largely abandoned but remains a site of interest for passing travelers and Crusaders.

1812

Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt rediscovers Petra, bringing it to the attention of the Western world.

1985

Petra is designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its historical and cultural significance.

Present Day

Petra remains one of the world's most famous archaeological sites and a popular tourist destination, with ongoing conservation efforts.

Interesting Facts

Hidden City

Petra was lost to the Western world for centuries until Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt rediscovered it in 1812.

Ancient Capital

Petra was once the thriving capital of the Nabataean Kingdom, flourishing due to its strategic position on trade routes.

Rock-Carved Architecture

Most of Petra’s iconic structures, including Al-Khazneh (The Treasury), were carved directly into sandstone cliffs.

Advanced Water System

The Nabataeans built an advanced water management system with canals, dams, and cisterns to store and distribute water in the desert.

Petra by Night

Petra offers a mesmerizing night experience where thousands of candles illuminate the Treasury, creating a breathtaking view.

UNESCO and Wonder of the World

Petra was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985 and was later named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007.

Architectural Marvel

Petra is renowned for its rock-cut architecture, where entire structures were carved directly into rose-red sandstone cliffs by the Nabataeans.

One of the most iconic structures, Al-Khazneh (The Treasury), stands 40 meters (131 feet) tall and showcases intricate Hellenistic and Nabataean design influences.

The city's architecture also features tombs, temples, theaters, and an advanced water management system, which enabled Petra to thrive in the arid desert environment.
