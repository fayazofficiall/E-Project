Great Wall of China

The world's longest man-made structure, spanning across mountains and valleys

WONDERS WORLD

A Monument of Chinese Ingenuity

The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications that were built across the historical northern borders of ancient Chinese states and Imperial China to protect against various nomadic groups from the Eurasian Steppe.

Built along an east-to-west line across the historical northern borders of China, the Great Wall was constructed to protect Chinese states and empires against the raids and invasions of nomadic groups. Several walls were built from as early as the 7th century BC, with selective stretches later joined by Qin Shi Huang (220–206 BC), the first Emperor of China. Little of the Qin wall remains today. Later on, many successive dynasties built and maintained multiple stretches of border walls.

Quick Facts
  • Location: Northern China
  • Built: 7th century BC - 17th century AD
  • Length: Approximately 21,196 kilometers (13,171 miles)
  • Materials: Stone, brick, tamped earth, wood
  • Dynasties: Multiple, notably Qin, Han, Ming
  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: 1987

History of the Great Wall

7th Century BC

The earliest walls were built during the Spring and Autumn Period and the Warring States Period as states began constructing defensive walls.

221-206 BC

Emperor Qin Shi Huang connected various sections to form the first unified Great Wall, using forced labor from soldiers and convicts.

206 BC-220 AD

Han Dynasty expands and reinforces the wall to protect the Silk Road trade routes.

1368-1644

Ming Dynasty undertakes large-scale rebuilding projects, creating much of the wall that exists today, using bricks and stone instead of earth.

1644

The Manchus breach the Great Wall and establish the Qing Dynasty. Wall-building ceases as they control territories on both sides.

19th-20th Century

The Great Wall falls into disrepair during this period as its military importance diminishes.

1987

The Great Wall is designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Present Day

Restoration efforts continue on sections of the wall, while it remains one of the world's most visited tourist attractions.

Interesting Facts

Incredible Length

The Great Wall stretches approximately 21,196 kilometers (13,171 miles) from east to west, crossing deserts, mountains, grasslands, and plateaus.

Human Cost

It's estimated that over a million workers died during the construction of the wall, earning it the nickname "the longest cemetery on Earth."

Space Myth

Contrary to popular belief, the Great Wall cannot be seen from the moon with the naked eye, although some sections can be visible from low Earth orbit.

Rice Mortar

Sticky rice was used as mortar in some sections built during the Ming Dynasty, making the wall extremely strong and durable.

Communication System

The wall had an integrated defense system with watchtowers, garrison stations, and signaling capabilities using smoke and fire to alert of enemy approach.

Geographical Challenges

Some sections of the wall were built along ridges with slopes as steep as 70 degrees, making construction extremely challenging.

Architectural Marvel

The Great Wall's architecture varies throughout its length, reflecting different dynasties and geographical contexts. The wall is not a single continuous structure but a collection of walls built by various dynasties.

The most well-preserved sections from the Ming Dynasty typically feature walls made of brick and stone with watchtowers positioned at regular intervals. These walls are generally 5-8 meters (16-26 feet) high and 4-5 meters (13-16 feet) wide.

The wall's construction adapted to the terrain, with different building techniques used for mountains, deserts, and plains. The architectural sophistication included drainage systems, facilities for collecting rainwater, and complex defensive designs.

Location

The Great Wall of China stretches across northern China, with popular visiting sections located near Beijing.
